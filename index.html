TIMELINE

January 2013
PM David Cameron favours and in/out referendum on the EU and this is included in the Conservative manifesto.
Since 1973 there have been eleven referendums held in the UK, the majority of them have been related to the issue of devolution.
May 2014
UKIP led by Nigel Farage wins 26% of the vote in European elections and becomes the UK's biggest representation in the European Parliament. The party’s key policy is to leave the EU.
UKIP stands for UK Independence Party.
May 2015
Conservative party win the general election.
June 2016
REFERENDUM RESULT: Leave campaigners win with 51.9% against and 48.1% for Remain and David Cameron resigns immediately.
The referendum turnout was 71.8%, with more than 30 million people voting. Brexit Secretary David Davis was confident of the result and made £1,000 betting Britain would vote Leave!
March 2017
New PM Theresa May triggers Article 50 starting a two year count down to Brexit.
Article 50 of the Treaty of Lisbon gives any EU member state the right to quit the EU. It gives the leaving country two years to negotiate an exit deal and once it's set in motion it can't be stopped except by unanimous consent of all member states.
June 2017
May calls an election but surprisingly loses seats. In the same month she starts formal negotiations with the EU.
July 2017
UK introduces European Union (withdrawal) Bill to revoke the 1972 European Communities Act), also known as the ‘Great Repeal Bill’. This copies EU laws into UK law.
This Bill has now passed through the House of Commons and Lords and is awaiting the final stage before it becomes law which is to receive Royal Assent.
December 2017
MPs force the Government to have a vote in the Commons on the final Brexit deal.
May promised there will be a Commons and Lords vote to approve whatever deal the UK and the rest of the EU agree. This vote was proposed to be a "take it or leave it" option after the deal was done. In theory, MPs could vote against and block Brexit but the EU side would need to agree too. The referendum result is not legally binding and the withdrawal agreement has to be ratified by Parliament.
March 2018
The UK and EU agreement starts to be formalised; agreements include dates for a transitional period and the status of UK citizens. This avoids a hard Brexit or a cliff edge.
This is the prospect of the UK crashing out of the EU with no deal at all. That would mean having no agreement on the terms of Britain's withdrawal or future trade relations, and could lead to serious disruption to businesses and individuals.
June-Oct-Dec 2018
Euopean Counil Summits.
The European Council defines the EU's overall political direction. It is not one of the EU's legislating institutions, so does not negotiate or adopt EU laws. Instead it sets the EU's policy agenda and actions to take.
January 2019
EU and British parliaments must ratify the withdrawal treaty before Brexit. To avoid last-minute jitters, getting that done at least two months ahead of time would help.
Deal needs to be agreed by at least 20 EU countries with 65% of the population.
March 2019
At 23:00 on 29 March 2019 the UK will leave the EU and the transition period begins, it will last to 31 December 2020.
It also allows time for the details of the new relationship to be fully hammered out. Free movement will continue during the transition period (as the EU wanted) and the UK will be able to strike its own trade deals but they won't come into force until 1 January 2021.
May-June 2019
European Parliament elections.
December 2020
End of the Brexit transition period. The negation period can be extended if all 27 EU countries agree.
